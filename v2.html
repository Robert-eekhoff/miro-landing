The problem has a name.
And decades of research behind it.

Sociologists call it cognitive labor: the invisible mental work of running a household. Not the cooking or cleaning, but the anticipating, planning, deciding, and tracking that happens before anyone lifts a finger.

Harvard sociologist Allison Daminger identified four stages of this work: anticipating what needs doing, identifying the options, making the decision, and monitoring the outcome. Her research, published in the American Sociological Review and expanded in her 2025 Princeton University Press book "What's on Her Mind," has been cited over 300 times by researchers worldwide.

Bestselling books like "Fair Play" by Eve Rodsky and "The Second Shift" by Arlie Hochschild brought this research to millions. The findings are consistent: this invisible work is real, it's measurable, and it falls disproportionately on one person in the household.

Every household app out there helps you do tasks. Check things off. Cross them out. But nobody built an app for the actual hard part: the carrying.
Until Sam.

Our story

Why I built Miiro

Miiro comes from the Japanese word miru (見る), meaning "to see."

I built it because the little things kept slipping through the cracks. Not because my wife and I weren't trying, but because no tool was built for how households actually work. The groceries nobody bought. The kindergarten pickup we both showed up to. The "I thought you were handling that" moments.

I read the research on cognitive labor and realized something: the hardest part of running a household isn't doing the tasks. It's carrying them. Remembering, planning, anticipating, tracking. And no app on earth was built for that.

So I built one. And I gave it an assistant named Sam who carries the load with you.

The name Miiro is a nod to both our names, rooted in the Japanese word miru, meaning "to see." Because the first step to sharing the load is making it visible.
