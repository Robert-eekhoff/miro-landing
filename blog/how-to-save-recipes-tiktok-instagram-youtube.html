You're scrolling TikTok at 11pm. Someone makes a creamy garlic pasta that looks incredible. You tap the bookmark icon. You tell yourself you'll make it this weekend.

You never make it.

Three weeks later, you vaguely remember the video but can't find it. It's buried under 200 other bookmarked videos about life hacks, cute dogs, and other recipes you also never made.

This is the recipe graveyard problem, and almost everyone who cooks has it.

The recipe graveyard problem

Social media has completely changed how we discover recipes. TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube are now where most people find new things to cook. The format is perfect for food content: short, visual, and mouthwatering.

But the saving part is broken.

You save 100 recipes across three platforms. You cook maybe 3 of them. The other 97 sit in various bookmark folders and saved collections, slowly getting buried by new saves. They become a graveyard of good intentions.

The core issue is that saving a recipe and actually cooking it are two completely different workflows. Saving is impulsive and takes one tap. Cooking requires planning, shopping, and deciding what to make on a specific day. No social media platform connects those two steps. They make saving easy and leave the rest up to you.

Here are four ways to save social media recipes, from the simplest to the most effective.

Method 1: Built-in save features

Every major platform has a built-in way to save content. TikTok has bookmarks (the flag icon). Instagram has saved collections (the bookmark icon on any post or reel). YouTube has playlists and the "Watch later" list.

Pros Free. Zero effort. One tap to save. You're already on the platform, so there's no extra step.
Cons Your saves are siloed. TikTok bookmarks don't talk to Instagram saves, which don't talk to YouTube playlists. There's no ingredient list, no recipe format, and no connection to shopping or meal planning. Saves get buried under new content. And because the platforms are designed to keep you scrolling (not cooking), your saved recipes disappear into algorithmic feeds where you'll rarely see them again.

Built-in saves work if you discover recipes on only one platform and have a small collection. But for most people, recipes come from everywhere, and the collection grows fast. Once you have more than a couple dozen saves, finding the one you want becomes a scrolling exercise in itself.

Method 2: Screenshots and Notes app

The classic workaround. See a recipe? Screenshot it. Or copy the caption and paste it into your Notes app. Some people create a dedicated note or folder for recipes.

Pros Works with any platform, any content. You own the content locally. It's better than nothing.
Cons Screenshots pile up fast and are nearly impossible to organize. They're not searchable (you can't search for "chicken" in a photo). There's no structured ingredient list, so you end up squinting at a screenshot in the grocery store trying to read what spices you need. Notes get messy and long. And screenshots from video recipes often miss key steps that were spoken but not shown on screen.

This method is popular because it's flexible, but it creates a new kind of mess. Instead of a digital bookmark graveyard, you have a camera roll graveyard. The recipes are technically saved, but practically lost.

Method 3: Dedicated recipe saver apps

There are several apps built specifically for saving recipes from the web. Apps like Flavorish, RecipeNotes, Pepper, and Plan to Eat let you paste a URL or share a link, and they'll extract the recipe into a clean format with ingredients, steps, and photos.

Pros Recipes are properly formatted with ingredients and steps separated out. You can search your collection. Everything is in one place regardless of where you found it. Some apps support importing from social media links.
Cons They solve saving, but that's where most of them stop. Your saved recipe collection is still disconnected from meal planning and grocery shopping. You save a recipe in one app, plan your week in your head (or another app), and write your grocery list somewhere else. The gap between "saved" and "cooked" stays wide open. Most of these apps are also designed for individual use, so if you cook with a partner, you're maintaining separate collections.

Dedicated recipe apps are a genuine step up from screenshots and bookmarks. If saving is your only problem, they work well. But if you've ever saved 50 recipes and still found yourself staring at the fridge saying "what should we make?", the saving wasn't the real bottleneck.

Method 4: Miiro's Toru

Toru is the recipe saver built into Miiro. It works like other recipe savers on the surface: paste any URL (including TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, and food blogs) and Toru extracts the recipe title, ingredients, and cooking steps into a clean format.

From saved to actually cooked

Here's the uncomfortable truth about recipe saving: the gap between saving and cooking isn't a saving problem. It's a planning problem.

You don't cook saved recipes because you forgot about them. You forgot about them because there's no step between "save" and "decide what to make for dinner tonight." That middle step is meal planning, and it's where most saved recipes go to die.

When you sit down on Sunday to plan your week's meals, you need to be able to browse your saved collection quickly, pick a few recipes, assign them to specific days, and generate a grocery list from the ingredients. If any of those steps require switching apps, manual copying, or digging through screenshot folders, the friction is high enough that you'll default to the same five meals you always make.

This is why the connection between saving and planning matters so much. A recipe saver that feeds into a meal planner that feeds into a grocery list removes the friction at every step. The recipe you saved at 11pm on a Tuesday actually shows up on your dinner table the following Thursday.

Bonus: turn your saved recipes into a weekly meal plan

Once you have a collection of saved recipes in one place, you can build a simple weekly routine that turns them into actual meals.

Pick a planning day. Sunday evening works for most couples. Take 10 minutes to scroll through your saved recipes together and pick 4 or 5 for the week. Let each person choose a couple of meals they're excited about.

Assign recipes to days. Think about your week ahead. Busy Tuesday? Pick something quick. Free Saturday? Try that more ambitious recipe you saved. Matching recipes to your schedule makes it far more likely you'll actually cook them.

Let the grocery list build itself. In Miiro, adding recipes to your meal plan automatically populates your shared grocery list with the ingredients you need. One of you can shop, or you can split the list and both grab items. Either way, when Wednesday night comes around and you're wondering what's for dinner, the answer is already decided, and the ingredients are already in your kitchen.

Save new recipes throughout the week. As you scroll TikTok, Instagram, or YouTube during the week, keep saving recipes that catch your eye. They'll be there waiting in your cookbook when the next planning session comes around. The collection grows over time, and so does the variety of your meals.

Frequently asked questions

Does Toru work with TikTok recipe links?

Yes. Paste a TikTok link into Toru and it extracts the recipe from the video's description or caption. If the creator included a written recipe, Toru will pull out the title, ingredients, and steps. You can edit any field after saving if you want to adjust something.

What about Instagram Reels and YouTube videos?

Same approach. Toru handles Instagram and YouTube links alongside regular food blog URLs. It extracts whatever recipe content is available from the page or description. For food blogs with structured recipe cards, the extraction is especially clean.

Can my partner see the recipes I save?

Yes. Toru saves recipes to your shared Miiro cookbook. Both partners in a Miiro household can browse, search, and add recipes to the meal plan. When your partner saves a recipe from Instagram at lunch, you can see it and add it to next week's meal plan that evening.

What if a recipe doesn't extract properly from a social media link?

Some social media posts don't include a full written recipe in the caption or description. In those cases, Toru extracts what it can, and you can manually fill in the rest. You can edit the title, ingredients, and steps after saving. It takes a minute or two, but you'll still end up with a properly formatted recipe in your shared cookbook.

Is Toru free?

Toru is built into Miiro. You can download Miiro and start saving recipes right away. The shared cookbook, meal planning, and grocery list features are all part of the app.
